The Bardo Experiment

OVERVIEW

For the Bardo Experiment we had to take the concept of "The Emptiness of the Atom" and create a comic styled story telling game/experience. We had to contemplate the literal science and philosophy of this concept, which lead to some complex and in depth conversations leading us to interesting ideas and various idea potentials. It was fun to working with difficult and complex logic/ideologies, then translating it into a new format.

This youtube short video is from a GIMM [Games, Interactive Media, and Mobile Technology] department instagram post. The post is a short 2 minute spotlight video of me discussing The Bardo Experiment, this video is part of a series of spotlights the department started doing to help showcase both its students and what the creative works coming out of the department.

Brandon Williams · Bardo Experiment OST

During this project there was lull in tasks needing my attention as many on mine were coding tasks set for the later end of the project. Another member made the base coding for several levels, I was to act like a stitcher and new systems coder, up until then my art pieces were largely done and I was on standby. This eventually lead to some strong feelings of imposter syndrome, which my team assured me there was no issues with my work. So with some freetime I looked into some Digital Audio Workstation's (DAW), landing on one called Cakewalk. In my freetime I started playing with Cakewalk and overtime, unprompted, created a soundtrack for The Bardo Experiment.

Please bare in mind that this was all down over the course of a month in a half starting with no experience. Almost exclusively done by ear, I add this to this page for a couple reasons. 1st, it ended up having a huge impact on the vibes and feeling of the scenes. 2nd, as a tool to represent initiative and desire to learn new tools and technology that can assist or improve a projects end goal or potential.

Scripts and Related tasking

I created various scripts such as an inventory system to check acquired energy and whether the player found the hidden atoms in each level; one called Atomic Check occurs on the final stage. It checks if all of the necessary hidden atoms have been discovered, or it will not let the player finish the game. This is achieved because the hidden atom of the final level only populates when all others are collected. Once the hidden atom of denial is collected, Denial's arm loses collision, freeing the player's path.

I also handled scripting for one of the two comic systems, specifically the one that allows the player to press next or back through the animated panels. One of the last scripts I created was for an animated credits sequence. It first moves through cards for each level and the logo for the level's primary creator, then finally transitions into a rolling credits scene with various titles and the names of the corresponding developers. I did this as a thank you and congratulations to the team in secret.

Though not scripting, I also set up a particle system on the final door of the game to give it an ethereal feel.

One of the biggest roles I played, though, was throughout the development process, where I would test and play with the various systems, as well as anything new added into the game, to ensure functionality and determine if any tweaking was needed to make new objects, artifacts, or scripts work well with the already established systems.
